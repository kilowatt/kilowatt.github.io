Section 1: Gaussian Filters and Edge Detectors

1.1: Finite Difference Operator

The finite difference filter is defined as: dX=[−1100] dY=[−1010]

While the second column/row isn't necessary for the finite difference filter for each filter respectively, I kept them to keep dimensions consistent. Consequently, the "partial derivative" with respect to x and y is defined as:

∂Img∂X=Img∗dX ∂Img∂Y=Img∗dY

And just like the continuous case, we can define the magnitude of the gradient as:

(∂Img∂X)2+(∂Img∂Y)2

Running it on the cameraman will give us the following results. The cutoff used for binarizing gradient magnitude is 0.3, and the order of images are running through dX, dY, mag, and binarized mag:



1.2: Derivative of Gaussian (DoG)

Running the vanilla finite difference filter will give us unstable results due to small disturbances that are not accounted for, and we want to make the outputs smoother. As a result, we will convolve the image with a Gaussian filter for a smoother output.

The Gaussian filter used here has a size of 15 and standard deviation of 2.5.

After dragging the Gaussian filter through, we arrive at the following output, with the same ordering and a threshold of 0.04 for binarization. Given that the filter that I dragged through is large, please increase the screen brightness in order to see the edges more clearly:

After convolving the image with a Gaussian filter, we see that the resulting outputs of the finite difference filter are much more smoothed out. The binarized gradient magnitude is also much thicker, since the Gaussian filter "spreads out" the effects of the edge into the surrounding pixels.

We can alternatively drag dX and dY through the Gaussian filter and derive a new filter, therefore we only need to drag a filter through the whole image once. Below are the new "Gaussian" dX and dY filters after being convolved by the simple finite difference filter:

Section 2: Fun with Frequencies

2.1: Blurring/Sharpening Images

Given that we have an image f, we can define sharpening the image as the following:

fsharp=f+α(f−f∗g)=f∗((1+α)e−αg)

Where g is a Gaussian filter and e is a unit impulse filter with the size of Gaussian Filter (1 at center and 0 everywhere else). We can then sharpen the image with this technique

2.3: Gaussian and Laplacian Stacks

A Gaussian stack is defined by a sequence of images in which it is continually being blurred by a Gaussian filter. A Lagrangian stack on the other hand, extracts the difference between 2 adjacent images within the Gaussian stack. While Gaussian stack continues to downsample the images, the Lagrangian stack preserves the high-level details of the image.

Forming the Gaussian and Laplacian stack on the example apple and orange images, I get the following, note that the Laplacian stack is normalized to capture the high-level differences
